# Freegle Helper — First Live Trial Summary

## What we tried

Mind in Brighton & Hove are relocating and had 47 items of office furniture to give away — desks, chairs, cabinets, a fridge, envelopes, clocks, a step ladder. They wanted these to go to local charities and community organisations, with collection on 7th-8th April.

We bulk-posted all 47 items using a new tool, then used an AI assistant to manage the incoming replies — acknowledging people, answering questions, checking dates, and gathering the information needed to decide who should get what. The AI handled the conversation; a human made the final allocation decisions.

During this trial the AI was operated manually, so responses were slower than they'd normally be. In production, replies would go out within minutes.

## What happened

**Friday afternoon — posts go live, first replies within the hour**

Two people replied about the fridge straight away. One got the collection dates wrong ("can collect today on Monday"). The other had forwarded it to friends at a local charity — acting as a go-between rather than wanting the fridge themselves.

Later that evening, a Freegler replied about the clocks and the step ladder, saying "not a charity so only if a charity doesn't ask for" — really considerate. And someone else replied to the desks, but their message was actually asking for computers — a standard template they send to lots of listings.

**Saturday morning — the replies we'd hoped for**

Someone from a local community farm replied about the trestle tables, explaining they needed them for their cafe area. They came back shortly after to ask about the step ladder too. When we responded, they confirmed they wanted all three tables plus the ladder and had transport sorted. Exactly the kind of reply we'd designed for.

Someone else asked how many envelopes were available — a simple question we could answer from the listing. But because we were running things manually, they had to wait hours and sent a follow-up: "Hope you can see my message." That really brought home why automation matters — a simple question shouldn't wait hours.

**Saturday lunchtime — first batch of responses go out**

We replied to everyone: confirmed dates, answered questions, asked about transport where needed, checked whether people were from charities. Every message was friendly but made no commitments — "we're still collecting interest and will confirm shortly."

People replied quickly after that. The person with wrong dates confirmed they couldn't make it — we kept them as a fallback. The envelope person turned out to be from a local charity and wanted about half. The community farm was all confirmed and ready to go.

## What we learned

**People don't read the post body.** The very first reply got the dates wrong. The Helper's first job is always to confirm the practical details.

**Some people are connectors, not collectors.** They forward your post to the right organisation. That's really valuable — treat them well.

**People self-select.** When the charity preference was clear in the post, one person explicitly said "only if no charity wants it." The Helper doesn't need to ask — just respect what they've told you.

**Template replies happen.** Someone replied about computers on a desk listing. Rather than ignoring them, we redirected them to what we actually had — they might genuinely want something.

**Speed matters.** When someone asks a simple question and has to chase after three hours, that's a bad experience. An automated Helper would answer in minutes.

**Don't assume geography.** When someone mentioned "Chesterfield" we nearly assumed they were hundreds of miles away. Turns out it was a local reference — they were 2.6 miles from the collection point. Always check the actual location data.

## What normally happens without help

We looked at similar situations in the past:

A furniture clearance in Greenwich got 88 replies. The offerer manually responded to 40 of them, tried to keep track of what was still available, and eventually gave up. The post expired without ever being formally completed.

A Samsung TV got 87 replies. The offerer replied to 2 people, picked one, and the other 85 never heard back.

That's what this is designed to prevent. Nobody should have to manage 88 conversations by hand, and nobody who takes the time to reply should be left in silence.

## Response patterns we saw

| What people did | How often | What the Helper does |
|----------------|-----------|---------------------|
| Clear, complete reply with all the info needed | 1 in 6 | Acknowledge and move straight to qualified |
| Asked a factual question before committing | 1 in 6 | Answer from listing data, then continue |
| Got the dates wrong | 1 in 6 | Gently clarify the actual arrangements |
| Acted as a go-between for a charity | 1 in 6 | Treat as a charity match, sort out logistics |
| Self-selected as backup ("only if no charity wants it") | 1 in 6 | Respect it — keep as fallback, don't re-ask |
| Sent a generic template not related to the item | 1 in 6 | Redirect to the actual items available |
